Unit Overview

What is a TED Talk?

TED stands for Technology, Entertainment, Design — but TED Talks have grown to cover every topic imaginable. The one rule? Ideas worth spreading. A TED Talk is a short, powerful speech (usually 4–18 minutes) where a speaker shares one big idea with the world — through story, evidence, and passion.



For your assessment, you will give a 3–5 minute TED Talk on a topic that genuinely excites you. You will be judged not just on what you say — but on how you say it, the story you tell, and the way you present your ideas visually.

"The best talks make you feel something before they make you think something."

✦ A TED Talk IS

  • A personal, passionate perspective
  • One central "big idea"
  • A story + evidence + emotion
  • Conversational and authentic
  • Rehearsed but not robotic

✦ A TED Talk is NOT

  • A formal essay read aloud
  • A PowerPoint with walls of text
  • A list of facts without feeling
  • Memorised word-for-word
  • About impressing with long words

Before You Begin
Find Your "Heart Sings" Topic
▼

Before you write a single word, you need the right topic. The most powerful TED Talks come from speakers who are obsessed with their subject. Audiences can tell the difference between someone who cares and someone who doesn't.

How to find your topic:
Ask yourself: What do I know about that most people don't? What problem do I wish the world would fix? What have I experienced that changed how I see things? What could I talk about for one hour without stopping?
Your topic should be specific, not general. Not "I will talk about social media" — but "I will argue that social media is secretly changing the way we feel about our own lives." Specific = powerful.
🌸 The Ikigai Method — Find Your Intersection

Ikigai is a Japanese concept meaning "reason for being." Answer all four quadrants — your TED Talk topic lives at their intersection.

Opening — First 30 Seconds
The Hook: Grab Them Instantly
▼
⏱ The first 30 seconds decide everything. If you lose the audience here, you may never get them back.

A hook is your opening move — a surprising statement, a question, a shocking fact, or the beginning of a story that makes the audience lean forward and think: "I need to know where this is going."

4 Types of Powerful Hooks:

🎯 The Bold Statement — Start with something surprising or controversial.
❓ The Rhetorical Question — Ask something your audience has never thought about.
📊 The Shocking Statistic — A number so surprising it demands attention.
🎭 The Story Opening — Drop us into a moment in medias res (in the middle of the action).
Example Hook — Bold Statement
"I want to tell you about the day I nearly failed my university entrance exam — and why that failure was the best thing that ever happened to me."
Example Hook — Shocking Statistic
"Every 40 seconds, someone in the world dies by suicide. That's over 2,000 people a day — and most of them never asked for help. I want to talk about why."
Do NOT start with "Good morning, my name is..." That is not a hook. Start with the idea — introduce yourself later, or let your ideas speak for themselves.

The Heart of Your Talk
Your Personal Story: Make It Real
▼

Great TED Talks are built on vulnerability and truth. Your personal story is what separates you from a textbook — it's the moment the audience stops listening and starts feeling. This is your most powerful tool.

The Story Arc — 5 Elements:

1️⃣ Setup — Who, where, when? Paint the scene.
2️⃣ The Problem / Turning Point — What went wrong, changed, or challenged you?
3️⃣ The Struggle — What did you do? What did you feel?
4️⃣ The Breakthrough — What changed? What did you learn or decide?
5️⃣ The Lesson — What does this mean for your audience? Why should they care?
Mini Story Example
"When I was 16, I sat in the car with my father in complete silence for three hours. He had just been told he would lose his job — a job he had held for 22 years. I didn't know what to say. Looking back now, I realise that silence taught me more about communication than any lecture ever could."
Storytelling Tips:
• Use specific details — not "I was sad" but "I sat in the dark, holding my phone, re-reading the message three times."
• Use dialogue — quote what people said. It brings scenes to life.
• Use present tense for action moments — "I walk in. The room is silent."
• Keep it to 60–90 seconds — powerful and focused.

The Body — The Evidence
Three Points That Convince
▼

After your story draws the audience in emotionally, your three points give your talk intellectual weight. Each point should support your big idea with a different type of evidence.

The Rule of Three:
The human brain loves groups of three. It feels complete and balanced. Structure each point the same way:

📌 State it — What is the point? (1 sentence)
🔍 Prove it — Evidence: fact, statistic, expert opinion, or research
🌍 Connect it — Why does this matter to your audience?
Each point should take about 45–60 seconds. Use a clear signpost phrase to move between them: "My first reason is...", "But that's not all — secondly...", "Finally, and perhaps most importantly..."
Example — Point 1 (State → Prove → Connect)
"First, sleep deprivation is an epidemic among young people. Research from the University of Michigan shows that 73% of students report feeling chronically tired. If you're sitting in this room right now feeling exhausted — you are not alone, and that is not normal."

Your Slides — Less is More
Visual Aids: Impress Without Words
▼

A TED Talk slide is not a page of notes — it is a visual punch. The best TED slides have almost no text at all. Your voice carries the message. The slide supports it visually.

The Golden Rule of TED Slides:

If your audience is reading your slide, they are not listening to you. Your job is to make slides that enhance what you say — not repeat it.
❌ Avoid This — "Classroom Style"
My Main Argument
  • • Point one is very important to note
  • • Point two has lots of data and research
  • • Please read this while I talk to you
  • • I am hiding behind my slides right now
  • • Bullet points show I ran out of ideas

Audiences read instead of listen. The speaker disappears.

✅ Aim for This — "TED Style"
HOPE.

One word. One image. One feeling. You do the talking.

"One powerful image is worth more than ten bullet points."
🖼️

Full-Bleed Images

Use one striking photograph that fills the entire slide. No borders. No text overlaid on the image.

🔢

Big Numbers

One statistic. Giant font. White on dark, or dark on white. "73%" on a slide is more powerful than a paragraph.

💬

One-Word Slides

Sometimes one word — "SILENCE." or "HOPE." — makes the audience feel exactly what you want.

📊

Simple Charts

One graph, clearly labelled. Show the trend only. Remove every element that does not carry information.

🎨

Colour & Contrast

Choose one or two brand colours. High contrast. Dark background + light text often reads better on stage.

🔤

Minimal Text

Maximum 6 words per slide. If you need more, split into two slides or — better — just say it out loud.

✦ Slide DO's

  • One idea per slide
  • High-quality, royalty-free images
  • Large, readable fonts (min 28pt)
  • Consistent colour theme
  • Plenty of empty space
  • 5–8 slides for a 3–5 minute talk

✦ Slide DON'Ts

  • Walls of bullet points
  • Clip art or low-res images
  • Reading off the slide
  • Animations that distract
  • Too many colours or fonts
  • Your script written on a slide

📐 Slide Planning Rule for a 4-Minute Talk

You should have approximately 5–8 slides. Think: 1 title slide, 1 for your hook/story, 1 per main point (3 slides), 1 for your closing message. That's 6–7 slides. Every slide should have a reason to exist.

Where to find free, high-quality images:
🌐 Unsplash.com — Beautiful, royalty-free photography
🌐 Pexels.com — Free photos and videos
🌐 Canva.com — Free slide templates + image library
🌐 Flaticon.com — Free icons and illustrations

Always choose images that feel right for the emotion of your point — not just the literal topic.

On Stage — Own the Room
Delivery: Body, Voice & Confidence
▼

Your words are only part of the message. Research suggests that 55% of communication is body language, 38% is voice tone, and only 7% is the words themselves. Delivery is everything on a stage.

The Speaker's Toolkit:

👁 Eye Contact — Look at different people. Hold eye contact for 3–4 seconds before moving on. Never stare at the screen or floor.

🗣 Voice Variation — Speed up for excitement. Slow down for emphasis. Use pauses — they are powerful, not awkward.

🙌 Gestures — Open hands, deliberate movement. No fidgeting. Let your hands breathe.

🚶 Movement — Move with intention. Step forward for importance. Step back to give the audience space.

😤 Nerves — Nervousness means you care. Before you speak, take three slow breaths. The audience wants you to succeed.
Practise standing up, not sitting down. Say your talk aloud at least 5 times before your assessment. Record yourself — it's uncomfortable, but it is the fastest way to improve.

After Your Talk — 10% of Your Grade
Oral Defense: Own Your Process
▼
🗣 The Oral Defense happens immediately after your presentation. Your teacher will ask you several questions. This is 10% of your total course grade.

The Oral Defense is not a test of your topic — it's a test of your process. Your teacher wants to know that you made genuine, thoughtful decisions when preparing your talk. If you built your presentation yourself and reflected on your choices, you will do well.

What the Oral Defense is really asking:

Your teacher is checking three things:

✅ This is your own work — you understand it fully and can talk about it naturally
✅ You made deliberate choices — you had reasons for your topic, story, slides, and structure
✅ You can reflect on your learning — you know what worked, what was hard, and what you would improve
The best answers are specific and honest. "I chose this image because it shows emotion without needing words, which fits my point about non-verbal communication" is far stronger than "I chose it because it looked nice."
Sample Question & Strong Answer
Q: "Why did you choose this PowerPoint design?"

"I chose a dark background with one large image per slide because I wanted my audience to focus on me, not on reading text. I watched a TED Talk where the speaker used one-word slides and it was very powerful, so I tried to do the same thing."
Practice Questions — Your teacher may ask:

📌 Why did you choose this topic? What personal connection do you have to it?
📌 Why did you choose that PowerPoint style or design?
📌 Where did you find your images? How did you decide which ones to use?
📌 How did you come up with your hook? Why did you choose that type?
📌 Why did you choose that particular personal story?
📌 How did you decide on your three main points?
📌 What was the most difficult part of preparing this talk?
📌 What would you change if you could do it again?
📌 What did you learn about yourself as a speaker through this process?
📌 How did you find the evidence / statistics / examples you used?
